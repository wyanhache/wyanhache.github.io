4.2 Buoyancy Force

Sea water > fresh water, cold water > hot water – Physics

Buoyancy Force  is the net difference between the two opposite forces pushing onto a submerged object.

  • pgh -> pressure at low is always higher than the pressure at high, pressure difference = force difference, uplifting force occurs

F b  = pgV

F b = the Weight of the displaced fluid (Archimedes principle)

  • When the object is stationary,

F b  = F g

  • When the percentage of submergence is in interest,

p object / p fluid = V displaced / V total

p fluid  and V displaced  are inversely proportionate.

  • W A : Apparent weight - In this case, Apparent Weight = F g  - F b  

3.1 Work and Power

POWERRRRR

  • Work is the amount of energy transferred/converted

Work = ∆E, F // d, or Fdcosθ

  • Scalar
  • Unit: Joule, or 1N*m

Work directions

  • If  0 ≤ θ < 90 (acute angle), you are giving energy and thus doing positive work
  • If θ = 90 (right angle), there is no work done
  • If 90 < θ ≤ 180 (obtuse angle), your energy is taken away, and thus doing negative work
  • Friction doesn’t always do negative work.

Power

  • The rate of doing work

Power = Work/Time or Force * Velocity

  • Scalar
  • Unit: watts, or 1J/s

5.1 Forms of Energy

ALL ENERGIES ARE SCALARS!!!!!!!1

  1. Kinetic Energy

  • The energy of motion.
  • Anything that has motion also has KE.

Formulas

  • KE = ½*mv 2

  • Wnet = ∆KE = KEf - KEi

  1. Potential Energy

  • Energy held by an object due to its relative position  to another object.

        Gravitational/Elastic Potential Energy

  • GPE - PE held corresponding to its height above a reference level
  • EPE - PE held as a result of a force deforming an object

E.g., Bungee Jump

  1. GPE -> KE = initial speeding up
  2. GPE+KE -> EPE = past the length of the rope, it starts to stretch and thus produces EPE
  3. The rope returns to its previous shape, through which EPE is converted into KE+GPE
  4. KE turns into GPE when my man goes up again
  5. Rinse and repeat…

GPE = mgh

Wgravity = -∆GPE

EPE = ½*ke 2   (k=elastic constant, e=extension)

Extending: KE-> EPE

Release: EPE -> KE

Relapse: KE -> EPE

Relapse 2: EPE -> KE

At the object’s original position, the KE is the largest

At the end of the extending position, the EPE is the largest

        Chemical Potential

  • The energy stored in chemical bonds
  • Bonds: the attraction force between atoms to form a molecule
  • Chemical reactions produce CPE.
  • E.g., photosynthesis, wood burning

  1. Mechanical Energy

  • A macroscopic energy related to motion and position
  • ME = GPE+EPE+KE

  1. Sound Energy

  • The energy released by the vibration of an object to produce a sound
  • All vibrating objects produce a sound except in vacuum.

  1. Thermal Energy

  • The energy released by the motion of particles, which generates thermal energy.

  1. Electric Energy

  • An energy resulting from the flow of electric charge/the energy an electric current carries.
  • Requires rotating to generate power.
  • From electrons, which carry a (-) charge.

  1. Radiant Energy (Electromagnetic/Light)

  • Energies from the electromagnetic spectrum
  • The electric field’s vibration is perpendicular to the magnetic field’s vibration, which is how the EM wave travels
  • Radio
  • Micro
  • Infrared
  • Visible
  • UV
  • X-ray
  • Gamma

  1. Nuclear Energy

  • The energy stored inside the nucleus of an atom

Fission

  • Clashing a neutron into an atom to break it into smaller parts, releasing energy
  • Used in power plants

        Fusion

  • Combining two atoms together to release energy
  • Not controllable

  • How does nuclear power plant work?

  1. Nuclear energy released (NE), boils a pot of water which evaporates into steam (TE)
  2. The steam drives a turbine (ME), which converts the energy into electricity (EE)
