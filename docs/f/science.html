Phylum Chordata

The three types of chordates: Lancelet, Tunicates, Vertebrates

  • Shared Characteristics

  • All chordates have these at some point in their life

Notochord

  • A flexible rod  that supports the back
  • A precursor to the vertebrate's backbone
  • Lancelet: keep notochord all their lives
  • Tunicates: only have a notochord at the larvae stage

Dorsal nerve cord

  • Runs down the back
  • E.g. human spinal cord
  • Connects the brain to nerves in the body

Throat pouches (pharyngeal slits)

  • Slits on the  throat area
  • Most only keep at the embryo stage ( degenerates after birth )
  • Some fishes keep all their life
  • The throat pouch develops into gill slits

Subphylum Vertebrata

  • Shared Characteristics

  • Backbone  sharing backbone and endoskeleton

  • Thermoregulation

  • Ectotherm:
  • Does not produce  internal heat
  • Body temperature is close to the environmental temperature
  • E.g. fish, amphibians, reptiles
  • Endotherm:
  • Produce internal heat to control  body temperature
  • Stable body temperatures , warmer than the environment
  • Uses fur, feather, or sweat glands  to achieve such
  • E.g. birds, mammals

5.1 Skeleton and Muscles

And finally, we have some bones to spare

Skeletons are structural frames of an animal’s body.

Functions

  • Shapes and supports  an animal
  • Protects  inner organs
  • Provides attachment  to the muscles , which allows for movement
  • Joints : places where two or more parts of the skeleton meet.

Types of Skeletons

Exoskeleton

  • Made of either calcium or chitin .
Advantages
  • Provides protection
  • Provides surface for muscle attachment
  • Provides a watertight interior  for moisturization
  • Has sensory organs to interact  with the environment
        Disadvantages
  • Does not include cells
  • It cannot grow along with the rest of the body
  • Adaptation:  molting  (seen in arthropods)
  • Exoskeletons are heavy
  • Such prevents the equipt animal from  growing very large

Endoskeleton

  • Made of living cells
        Advantages
  • Relatively light
  • Such allows the equipt animal to grow large
  • Grows with  the animal
        Disadvantages
  • When only the endoskeleton is equipt, it has a lesser level  of protection

Hydroskeletons

  • a skeleton without any hard parts.
  • Instead, it is a fluid-filled cavity   surrounded by muscles
  • Helps the equipt animal with shape and locomotion .

5.2 The Nervous System

E-l-e-t-r-i-c-i-t-y (-- Electro Wizard)

Functions of the NS

  • Detect signals  in the environment
  • Process  signals
  • Reacts  to signals

Stimulus: a signal that causes an animal to react in some way

  • E.g., touch, sound, smell, taste, etc.

Response: an animal’s reaction to a stimulus

Types of Neurons

Neuron: a nerve cell

  • Impulse : information that travels as an electrical messenger in a nerve cell.
  • Sensory : nerve cells that detect stimuli
  • E.g., olfactory, thermoreceptors, mechanoreceptors, etc.
  • Relay : pass information between neurons
  • E.g., spinal cord
  • Motor : nerve cells that carry response information to muscles and other organs.
  • Usually in companion with muscle cells

Nervous System in Animals

  • Well-developed brains

Sense Organs

  • Brain: an organized group of neurons  in the head of an animal with  bilateral symmetry .
  • Receive  information
  • Interpret  information
  • Control response
  • Eyes: simple and compound eyes
  • Antennae : senses touch, smell, taste, etc.
  • Nasal passage : olfactory sense
  • Lateral line : detects movement in water
  • Tympanic membrane  (eardrum)
  • Sound causes the membrane to vibrate, transmitted to the brain through the middle and inner ear.

6.1 The Digestive Matters

🤮- Food  is the go-to for animals to getting energy for their cells

Types of Eaters

  • Carnivores : eat meat
  • Herbivores : eat plant (average vegan)
  • Detritivores : eat decaying animals or plants.

The Feeding Process

  • Mouthpart : tearing, chewing, sucking food
  • E.g., grasshopper has sharp mouth, butterfly sucks plant juices, hummingbird has a long, forked tongue

Carnivores

  • Pointed teeth for chewing meat
  • E.g., canine teeth in humans
  • Up and down chewing motion to chop their food

Herbivores

  • Broad and flat teeth for chewing plants
  • Side to side motion in jawbones to chop their food

Omnivores

  • A blend of both pointed and flat teeth.

Filter Feeders

  • Drains food from water.

The Digesting Process

  • There are two types of digestion: physical/mechanical and chemical.

Intracellular Processes

  • Digest food inside specialized cells
  • E.g., sponges, which use collar cells and jelly-like cells

Extracellular Processes

  • Involves the digestive system

        Simpler

  • Cnidarians, flatworms, etc.
  • One opening through which the food enters and exits
Case Study: Planarians
  • Feeding tube (pharynx)
  • Releases enzymes outside which breaks down the food partially
  • Intake

Complexer

  • Two antipodal openings
  • Special area for the digestion and absorption to take place
  • E.g., mollusks, fish, birds, etc.

                Fish (again)

  • Two openings
  • More efficient digestion
  • Digested food does not mix with undigested

                Birds

  • Have no teeth
  • Has crop to store food in long flights
  • Stomach: long, thin wall containing acid and enzymes
  • Glizzard: thick-walled, filled with stone, squeezes and grinds food

  • Four compartments
