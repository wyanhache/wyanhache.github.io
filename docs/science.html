3.2.2 How Do Seeds Become New Plants

My seed inside your madre

Seed

  • Inside a seed is a partially developed plant .
  • If a seed lands in an area where conditions are favorable, the plants grows out

Seed Structure:

  • Embryo :
  • Young plant develops from the zygote
  • Has seed root (radicle), seed stem (hypocotyl ; below the cotyledon ), and seed leaf (cotyledon/plumule)
  • Stored food :
  • Some plants do not store food inside the cotyledon
  • Seed coat :
  • Cover and protect  the embryo and the stored food
  • Allow the seed to remain inactive for long

Seed Germination

  • The early growth stage of the embryo when the embryo begins to grow again and pushes out of the seed.
  • Germination Stages:
  1. Seed absorbs water
  2. Seed use stored food  to grow
  3. Embryo root grows downward
  4. Then Leaves and stems grow upward

Seed Dispersal

  • Seed dispersal is the scat tering of seeds

  • Way of dispersal

  • By animals
  • Fruit ingestion, stick to fur
  • By water:
  • Coconut seeds, propagate
  • By wind:
  • Wind disperses light seeds such as dandelion, willows
  • Less efficient than other ways of dispersal
  • By plant themselves:
  • Plant shoot out the seeds (beans, SQUIRTING CUCUMBER)

3.4.1 What are the stages of a plant life cycle?

HOW DO THEY HAVE SPORES HUH

  • Two different stages
  • Sporophyte  stage ( diploid  multicellular stage)
  • Meiosis produces spores  (one set chromosome)
  • Spores will grow into the gametophyte stage
  • Gametophyte  stage ( haploid  multicellular stage)
  • Produces sperm or egg (gametes)
  • Sperm and egg fertilization to zygote; zygote mitosis into sporophyte
  • Classification based on life cycle:
  • Annuals:
  • Plants that complete a life cycle within one growing season
  • E.g. wheat, tomatoes, cucumbers, marigold, petunias
  • Biennials:
  • Plants complete a life cycle within two years
  • Year 1:  germinate  and grow roots, short stems, and leaves
  • Year 2: lengthen stems, grow new leaves, produce flowers and seeds
  • E.g. parsley, celery, foxgloves
  • Perennials:
  • Plant that lives for more than two years
  • E.g. peonies, asparagus, evergreens, pines, oak trees, honeysuckle
  • Most have woody stems

3.5 Plant Response and Growth

This is why you need good sleep

  • Tropism : a growth response towards or away from a stimulus  by a plant
  • Positive : growing toward the stimuli
  • Negative : growing away from the stimuli

Types ( Response to _____)

  • Thigmo-  (touch). E.g., tomato vines coil around any object they touch for support.
  • Gravi- (gravity). E.g., roots grow towards gravity (+ tropism), and stems grow upwards towards the light (- tropism).
  • Photo-  (light). E.g, Stems, leaves, and flowers show positive tropism for photosynthesis.
  • Hormone: chemical that affects how plants grow/develop.
  • Auxin : type of hormone, that regulates growth of plant cells + response to light.
  • Auxin builds up on the opposite side of the light for the stem to bend towards the light.

Photoperiodism

  • Plant’s response to seasonal changes in day/night length.
  • The amount of darkness a plant receives determines the time of flowering in many plants.
  • Critical night length  (CRL): # hours in darkness determining whether the flower will flower.

Night Length (bloom time)

E.g.,

Short-day : they will only flower when the night length > CNL. (FA/WI)

Chrysanthemums (菊花）, plum blossoms

Long-day : NL < CNL (SP/SU)

Irises, lettuce

Day-neutral : not sensitive to light and dark. (Year-round)

Tomatoes, rice

  • Dormancy : when an organism’s growth or activity  stops
  • Helps survive freezing temperatures and lack of liquid water.
  • In fall, leaves stop making chlorophyll and begin to turn color
  • Sugar and water are transported out  of leaves

ANIMA

  • Phylogenic tree  - a tree showcasing how interrelated animals are through body structures, evolutionary descent, DNA, etc.

Symmetry

Radial Symmetry

  • Many imaginary lines  can be drawn to divide the object into two mirror images
  • Live in water
  • Sense in all directions
  • Have no distinct front or back ends
  • Do not have heads  (no head?)
  • Complex body plan with tissues and usually with organ system

Bilateral Symmetry

  • Only one imaginary line  can be drawn to divide the object into two mirror images
  • Streamlined  body w/head in front
  • Move quickly
  • Have sense organs
  • Large and more complex, have organ system

Asymmetrical

  • No symmetry at all (shame)
  • Simple body organization , no tissues though

Sponges

  • Phylum Porifera
  • Irregular shaped (asymmetrical)
  • Body with pores
  • Lacking tissues and organs
  • Live in both sea/fresh water

Structure

  • Porocyte:
  • Makes pore
  • Allows water, food, oxygen  to enter sponge
  • Central cavity (spongocoel):
  • Where food is filtered/digested
  • Collar cell (choanocyte):
  • Lines the central cavity
  • Have whip-like structures that help to move the water
  • Traps food  from water
  • Amoebocyte (jellylike cells):
  • Digest  and distribute food
  • Carry away wastes
  • Form gametes
  • Osculum:
  • Water and waste flow out
  • Larvae  can be carried away by water flow
  • Spicules:
  • Lie between the outer and inner cell layers
  • Tough, support the sponge’s body
  • Some can produce  irritating substances for defense

Obtaining Oxygen/Food
