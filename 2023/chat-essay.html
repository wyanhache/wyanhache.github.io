Determining the Greater Risk that ChatGPT Content Poses to Correct Information

Introduction

Imagine if you text a friend for answers to the physics worksheet you didn’t bother to do, you copied his answers and handed it in - and the answers were all wrong. Your hideous score is a consequence of misinformation, something that everyone has to combat on the internet vigorously these days. In November 2022, an AI-powered language model called ChatGPT emerged. It and its successor, GPT-4 [1] , provide astonishing answers to user-generated prompts. This report attempts to expose how ChatGPT is already incorporated in diffusing incorrect information, what possibilities are there for mitigation, and what has already been achieved in countering such illicit doings.

Why? - The Extant Effects of Misinformation by ChatGPT

First and foremost, ChatGPT has been utilized for contorting valid information into incoherent ideas. In the introduction blog post for ChatGPT, OpenAI stated, “ChatGPT sometimes writes plausible-sounding but incorrect or nonsensical answers” (OpenAI). This outlines the fact that ChatGPT can make up statements from nowhere and present them in a believable format for a user base of over 100 million people (Hu). In reality, ChatGPT only produces information that is only about “50% - 70% of the time” (De Witte), presenting more room for misinformation by ChatGPT being spread around the massive web.

Case Study: Princeton Computer Science Course

Such themes have already been expressed on school campuses. Princeton computer science professor Arvind Narayanan noted, “I gave [ChatGPT] q[uestion]'s from my infosec [2]  final exam at Princeton that require critical thinking. I had to read some of the answers three times before feeling confident I was reading nonsense” (Narayanan). Such an instance manifests the intricateness and nonsensical edge of the text transformer, with its responses being far away from correct. In a hypothetical scenario, CS students at Princeton may use ChatGPT for their course, only to generate ludicrous answers that neither benefit their professor nor themselves; the above case is only of the infinite potential troubles. “New AI releases tend to look impressive based on examples that go viral on Twitter. But [...] [f]or many applications, even a 10% failure rate is too high”, Narayanan continued to relate.

Case Study: Banned on StackOverflow

Such a dilemma has also affected StackOverflow, the largest community forum dedicated to answering computer science questions. Moderators officially banned ChatGPT and its derived answers in December 2022, as they express their concerns in a thread: “While the answers [...] have a high rate of being incorrect, they typically look like they might be good and the answers are very easy to produce. There are also many people trying out ChatGPT to create answers, without the expertise or willingness to verify that the answer is correct prior to posting” (Makyen). This policy intuitively illustrates the level of harm ChatGPT may bring to a community similar to StackOverflow: By unaware and inexperienced persons contributing incorrect answers to the community, it will result in confoundment from users seeking actual correct answers from the site, leading to impairments that may exceedingly disrupt the professional sphere of front-end development. This continues to stand true in countless professions using ChatGPT - if they are affected by misinformation, humongous gashes may be left in their progress.

What? - What are Feasible Solutions for Mitigation?

        Secondly, aside from distinguishing the areas already influenced by ChatGPT’s low correctness, it is important to discuss theoretical mitigations to the damages that can be done. Researchers have attempted to brainstorm methods for further expediency from ChatGPT.

Conducted Research

        For example, OpenAI itself has investigated potential disinformation [3]  uses of its product in a joint study with Stanford IO [4] , in which they discussed how “The potential of language models [...] may provide distinct advantages to propagandists” (Goldstein, et al., Emerging Threats , 1).  Yet, the paper also showcases possible ways to combat such campaigns, such as “ AI developers build models that are more fact-sensitive”, “Entities that rely on public input take steps to reduce their exposure to misleading AI content”, and “Institutions engage in media literacy campaigns” (Goldstein, et al., Forecasting misuses ).  The above collection of concepts revolved around two main ideas: public perception and further development.

Discussing Plausible Solutions

For the former, the report argues that the public must be promptly educated about the possibility of misinformation [5]  from this new technology to avoid falling victim to such cases. This would require prodigious help from education institutes, national governments, etc., which may lead it to be less feasible in places of limited awareness outreach.

The latter gives the responsibility to developers that interacts with the language model first-hand: if they train the text transformer on less biased and more truthful data, users can enjoy the prowess of ChatGPT without deceitful and incorrect information. However, developing bots that take in more credible and abundant sources takes a substantial amount of time. It took GPT-3 10 years [6]  to complete training (Brown, et al., 46); more expansive technology will therefore require greater power and time to develop.

How? - What Has Already Been Done, What is to Be Done

        Thirdly, it is important to recognize the efforts already in place to intervene in the mass spread of incorrect information generated by ChatGPT across the web. Through the aid of countless developers and users of ChatGPT, the acumen for AI misinformation has greatly expanded to serve a better future for AGI [7] .

Classifying AI-Generated Content

Many engineers in the AI sphere, including OpenAI, have created content classifiers to distinguish AI-generated text. They attain this by checking for verbosity, fluency, and word choice unique to language models like ChatGPT. Though tools like this are usually prominent in academic fields, it is extremely useful to recognize AI-generated conspiracy theories and propaganda for popular awareness. In January 2023, OpenAI released its own classifier for AI-generated content. It is not completely reliable, as stated: “ Our classifier is not fully reliable [...] [it] correctly identifies 26% of AI-written text (true positives) as ‘likely AI-written,’ while incorrectly labeling human-written text as AI-written 9% of the time (false positives)” (Kirchner, et al.), yet there are alternatives such as GPTZero [8] , and this but an initiative.

Utilizing the Chatbot for Good Intents

        ChatGPT (and GPT-4) have enticed major and minor entities to create AI-oriented products. This is a time when vicissitude in technology trends is imminent, and one should be encouraged to create imaginative and creative ideas revolving around this concept.

In March 2023, OpenAI introduced API outlets for ChatGPT and Whisper [9]  (Brockman), with Snapchat and Duolingo already integrating them (Heath; Duolingo Team), and even commonplace developers such as Fireship [10]  already expressing intuitive applications. Therefore, in this new age of technological exploration, do creative things with groundbreaking products instead of trying to break the web with it.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the disruptive language model ChatGPT brings new concerns about the correctness of its generated information, such as disrupting professional spheres and creating widespread disinformation. Many mitigations have already been proposed, such as popular education on factual ideas and augmentation of model development. Finally, it is needed for ChatGPT users to use the technology for good intentions instead of for wrongful deeds. As internet personality Tom Scott once said, “Try and make sure what you are working on is pushing us in the right direction [...] And even if they don’t last, try to make sure they leave something positive behind.” [11]
